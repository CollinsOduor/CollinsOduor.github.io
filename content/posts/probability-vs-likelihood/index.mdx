I have seen on the internet these two terms being used interchangebly. But do they actually mean the same thing?

Perhaps their only similarity is that they are both used as a measure of uncertainty. Beyond that, they are
fundamentally different and I show why below.

Probability tells us how regularly we should expect a random variable (or event) to occur. More scientifically, it is the ratio of the the
count of event E to the total count in the sample space SS.

Likelihood, on the other hand, is the measure of the extent to which we can prove the null(or alternative) hypothesis.

Take this example:

> A multiple choice test with 10 questions is administered to a class. The scores of student A are taken as [F,S,F,F,S,F,S,F,F,S]; where F denotes Failure,
> and S denotes Success.

_Note that the sample space has 11 possible events (assuming we are interested in the number of Successful events)_

In a strict game of chance, the probabilities can be computed using Bernoulli Trials as below:

```math
P(S=0) = 0.0009765625
P(S=1) = 0.009765625
P(S=2) = 0.0439453125
...
P(S=10) = 0.0009765625
```

In this case the probability is given for each event.

I could further theorize that the a student who gets less than 5 questions correct is unlucky. Coming up with the theory is easy;
convincing others to buy my idea is much harder. Part of the convincing game involves arguing for and/or against competing scenarios.

Take the two theories below:

> H0: Student A is serially unlucky; geting less than 5 questions correct is a continuation of this trend  
> H1: The test was not standardized; even students who have aced similar tests before failed this time

In a case where more than half of the class got less than half of the questions correct, we could reject the null in favour of the
more plausible alternative. It is also worth noting that likelihoods do not mean much on their own. Rather it is the relative likelihoods
that are reported - H1 is more likely than theory H0, or the other way round.
